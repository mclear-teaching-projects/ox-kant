Week 4 - The Autonomy of Sense

Date Wed 17 May 2017 Tags intellectualism / intuition / sensibilism / transcendental deduction

Kant distinguishes between to basic faculties—sensibility and the intellect (or understanding & reason). However, a significant and influential swath of Kant scholarship nevertheless construes Kant as claiming that basic sensory representations—-i.e. intuitions—-are in part dependent on acts of the intellect for their formation. This week we discuss whether, and to what extent, such claims of dependence could be correct.

Core Readings

  • Kant: Transcendental Aesthetic; B-Deduction
  • McLear, Two Kinds of Unity in the Critique of Pure Reason

Recommended Readings

  • Messina, Kant on the Unity of Space and the Synthetic Unity of Apperception
  • Onof & Schulting, Space as Form of Intuition and as Formal Intuition
  • Grüne, Sensible Synthesis and the Intuition of Space
  • Gomes, Naive Realism in Kantian Phrase
  • Longuenesse, Synthesis Speciosa and Forms of Sensibility (ch. 8 of Kant and the Capacity to Judge)
